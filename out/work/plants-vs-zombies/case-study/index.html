Plants vs. Zombies

A UX case study tracing how progression in Plants vs Zombies: Battle for Neighborville evolved from loot boxes to Prize Maps, and what that shift meant for player rewards and engagement.

Context

Overview

As Lead of the UX team on Plants vs Zombies: Battle for Neighbourville, one of our major challenges was redesigning the progression and rewards system. The previous title, Garden Warfare 2, used a loot box-based economy that had become increasingly misaligned with player expectations for transparency and fairness. With rising global criticism toward randomized rewards, we had a clear UX goal: replace the opaque loot box system with a transparent, player-friendly progression system that maintained excitement, improved clarity, and empowered user choice.

The problem

Problems

We identified these issues through a mix of qualitative and quantitative research. Community forums, internal telemetry and support tickets revealed consistent player frustration in specific areas. Together these insights made it clear which issues in Garden Warfare 2 would need addressing.

Progression was opaque

Players could not predict what they would receive.

Player agency was low

Rewards were randomized, causing frustration.

Perception of pay-to-win

Monetization tied to loot boxes undermined the game’s family-friendly appeal.

Poor reward pacing

Players either hit droughts or spikes, making the experience inconsistent.

Goals

UX Goals

We aimed to design a new system that:

  • Provided clarity and transparency in progression.
  • Increased player agency and reward anticipation.
  • Supported seasonal content and encouraged long-term engagement.
  • Removed monetization friction while keeping rewards exciting.
Process

UX Methods That Shaped the Solution

To ensure the Prize Map system was grounded in user-centered design, we applied a range of UX research and design methods throughout development:

Competitive Analysis

Goal

Understand how other live-service games (like Fortnite, Apex Legends, and Overwatch) handled progression and seasonal content.

Outcome

Identified that successful games were moving toward transparency, limited-time events, and visual progression systems — all core inspirations for our approach.

Usability Testing

Goal

Playtest-Cloud, internal QA sessions, Think-Aloud protocols.

Focus areas

  • Can users understand how to progress?
  • Do they feel they have meaningful choices?
  • Is the visual layout too overwhelming or unclear?

Outcome

Led to simplification of node layouts, improved labeling, and onboarding for new users.

Sentiment Analysis

Tools

Manual parsing of Reddit threads, Discord comments, and support tickets from Garden Warfare 2.

Focus areas

Identifying emotional triggers and recurring complaints around randomness, fairness, and grind.

Outcome

Validated our direction and reinforced the need for visible, predictable rewards.

The solution

The Prize Map System

We introduced Prize Maps — seasonal progression boards where players could see their path to rewards and choose which nodes to unlock using in-game currency earned through gameplay. This system combined visual clarity, strategic choice, and long-term engagement.

Full visual clarity

Players could preview every available reward and the path to reach them.

Branching choices

Players chose their route through the map, giving them control over their rewards.

Seasonal themes

Monthly maps introduced new cosmetics and characters, adding novelty and structure.

Visible progress & milestones

Each step showed clear progression feedback, encouraging retention.

Impact

Outcomes

Increased satisfaction

The redesigned system gave players more control and a clearer sense of progress, which led to a noticeable improvement in sentiment. Many players felt more motivated to engage consistently, with fewer complaints about randomness or unfairness compared to previous titles.

Positive press

Players praised the transparency and fairness of the system in reviews and community feedback.

No pay-to-win backlash

With no randomized monetization tied to performance, we maintained trust with players and parents alike.

Reflection

Reflection

The shift from loot boxes to Prize Maps was more than a feature redesign — it was a shift in corporate philosophy. We moved from exploiting randomness to respecting player choice. It taught us the value of transparency and how reward systems can either build or break trust.

By centering UX principles in this redesign, we built a progression system that felt fun, fair, and forward-looking.
